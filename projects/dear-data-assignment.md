## What Is Dear Data?

*Dear Data* is a year-long project by data visualization designers **Giorgia Lupi** (Italy) and **Stefanie Posavec** (UK). Every week, they each collected data about their own daily lives, hand-drew a visualization of that data on a postcard, and mailed it across the Atlantic to each other. The catch: neither knew what the other would draw, and the only way to understand a postcard was through a hand-drawn **legend** on the back.

The project asks a deceptively simple question: **what does your life look like as data?**

It's not about perfect graphs or polished software. It's about paying close attention to your own experience ‚Äî and then inventing a visual language to share it.

## The Assignment

Over the next week, you will become a Dear Data collector. You'll track one aspect of your daily life, hand-draw a visualization of what you find, and share it with your classmates.

This is an **analog, personal, creative** assignment ‚Äî no laptops required for the main artifact. The goal is not a beautiful chart. The goal is to notice something about your life you might otherwise overlook, and figure out how to show it to someone else.

## Step 2: Commit to Your Topic (Monday in Class)

After the class vote, you'll choose one of the three selected topics. This is your topic for the week ‚Äî you'll collect data on it from **Monday through Friday** (5 full days of collection).

## Step 3: Collect Your Data

There is no required format for data collection during the week. Use whatever works for you:

- A small notebook you carry
- A note in your phone
- A simple text file or spreadsheet
- A recurring phone alarm to prompt you

**Tips for good collection:**
- Record in the moment, not from memory at the end of the day
- Capture more detail than you think you'll need ‚Äî you can always simplify
- Note things that surprise you or feel hard to categorize ‚Äî those are often the most interesting data points
- Look for both **quantity** (how much, how many, how long) and **quality** (how it felt, what it meant)

---

## Step 4: Draw Your Postcard

At the end of the week, create your visualization **by hand** on a piece of paper approximately postcard size (4√ó6 inches), or on a larger sheet if you prefer ‚Äî whichever lets you show your data clearly.

**Your postcard must include:**

1. **A visualization** of your week's data ‚Äî this can be any visual form you invent. It does not have to be a bar chart. Consider: symbols, shapes, colors, patterns, grids, maps, timelines, spirals, anything.

2. **A legend / decoder** (on the back, or in a corner) that explains how to read your visualization. Someone who has never seen your data should be able to decode it using your legend alone.

**Questions to ask yourself while designing:**
- What do I want someone to notice first?
- What visual variable (shape, size, color, position) best represents this kind of information?
- What surprised me most about my data ‚Äî can I make that visible?
- Does my legend explain everything a stranger would need to read this?

There are no wrong visual choices here. You are **inventing a visual language**. Be creative, be yourself.

## Step 5: Gallery Walk (Following Monday, in Class)

We'll display all postcards in the classroom, grouped by topic. You'll have time to:

- Walk around and try to **decode** your classmates' postcards using their legends
- Spend extra time with postcards that share your topic ‚Äî what did they track that you didn't? What visual choices did they make that you never considered?
- Leave a sticky note (or verbal comment) on one that surprised or intrigued you

After the walk, we'll discuss as a class: when multiple people track the same thing, why do the visualizations look so different? What does that tell us about data and representation?

This is not a critique ‚Äî it's a conversation.

## Step 6: Written Reflection (Due Monday)

Submit a short written reflection (approximately **200‚Äì300 words**) answering the following:

1. **What did you track, and why did you choose it?**

There are no right answers here. This is a space for honest reflection.

## A Note on Why We're Doing This

Before we dive into tools, software, and datasets, it's worth asking: *what even is data?*

Everything in your life can be observed, recorded, and represented ‚Äî your habits, your moods, your choices, your movements. Data isn't just something that lives in spreadsheets or databases. It's an abstraction of lived experience.

When you design your own system for encoding that experience visually, you encounter all the fundamental tensions of data visualization: What do you include? What do you leave out? What does the visual form imply that the numbers don't? Who is your audience, and what do you want them to feel?

These questions are at the heart of DATA 271. This assignment is just the beginning of asking them.
